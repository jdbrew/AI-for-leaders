What is Artificial Intelligence?

This page provides a strategic overview of AI for leaders. The following sections demystify core concepts, review essential tools, highlight critical risks, and teach practical skills to help you navigate the evolving AI landscape effectively and responsibly.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a broad field of computer science focused on creating systems that can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. This includes abilities like learning, reasoning, problem-solving, perception, and language understanding.

Think of AI not as a single entity, but as an umbrella term for a range of methods and tools that enable machines to simulate human-like intellectual tasks. The goal is to create technology that can operate intelligently and autonomously to augment human capability.

Understanding the Types of AI

AI isn't a monolith. For leaders, it's helpful to categorise it by its function. Click through the tabs below to explore the most relevant types of AI in the business and healthcare landscape today.

Generative AI

This is the category that has captured the public's imagination. Generative AI **creates new, original content**. This could be text, images, music, code, or even video. It learns from vast amounts of existing data and then generates novel outputs.

Example:

"Draft a communication plan for the rollout of a new digital training module for clinical staff."

Search AI

This goes beyond traditional keyword search. Search AI understands the **intent and context** behind your query to provide more relevant and comprehensive answers, often summarising information from multiple sources into a coherent response.

Example:

"Summarise the latest NICE guidelines on diabetes management and highlight any changes from the previous version."

Conversational AI

These are systems designed to understand and respond to human language in a natural, interactive way. This includes everything from simple patient-facing chatbots to sophisticated digital assistants for staff.

Example:

A website chatbot that helps staff find the correct HR policy document by asking them a series of questions.

Automation AI

This type of AI is focused on automating repetitive, rule-based tasks to increase efficiency, reduce human error, and free up staff for more complex work. It is often a core component of Robotic Process Automation (RPA).

Example:

An AI system that automatically categorises incoming patient feedback into themes like 'scheduling', 'clinical care', or 'facilities'.

A Look at Common AI Tools

The market is filled with AI tools, each with unique strengths. Here is a breakdown of some of the most prominent options to help you understand their capabilities and typical use cases. Hover over a card for more detail.

ChatGPT (OpenAI)

Generative, Conversational

Highly creative and versatile, excellent for brainstorming, drafting content, and summarising text.

STRENGTHS:

Creative writing, code generation, general brainstorming.

WEAKNESSES:

Knowledge cut-off dates, potential for "hallucinations" (making things up).

Gemini (Google)

Generative, Search, Conversational

Access to real-time information from the web makes it powerful for research, planning, and analysis.

STRENGTHS:

Real-time data access, strong analytical capabilities, multimodal input.

WEAKNESSES:

Can sometimes be less fluidly conversational than competitors.

Microsoft Copilot

Generative, Automation

Deeply integrated into Microsoft 365, ideal for boosting productivity within your existing workflows.

STRENGTHS:

Context-aware within Office apps, great for summarising emails, creating presentations.

WEAKNESSES:

Value is tied to the Microsoft ecosystem; less useful as a standalone tool.

Perplexity AI

Search, Conversational

Acts as a "conversational search engine," providing direct answers with citations and sources.

STRENGTHS:

Excellent for research and fact-checking, provides sources to reduce hallucinations.

WEAKNESSES:

Not designed for creative or open-ended content generation.

Navigating Risks & Data Privacy

While AI offers incredible opportunities, responsible leadership requires awareness of the risks. Understanding the distinction between free and paid tools is paramount for protecting sensitive information.

Key Cautions

  • !

    Accuracy & Hallucinations

    AI models can and do make things up. All outputs, especially factual claims, MUST be verified by a human expert.

  • !

    Bias

    AI models learn from real-world data, which contains human biases. These can be reflected and amplified in the AI's output.

  • !

    Over-Reliance

    AI should be a tool to augment human intelligence, not replace critical thinking. Human oversight is essential.

Security & Data Privacy

This is a critical distinction for any organisation. Your approach must be guided by the sensitivity of the information involved.

Free Public AI Tools

When using a free tool, you are often "paying" with your data. Prompts may be used to train the model. There is no guarantee of privacy.

Prohibited for any confidential or patient-identifiable information.

Paid / Enterprise AI Tools

With enterprise solutions, your organisation is the customer, not the product. These tools offer contractual guarantees that your data remains private and is not used for model training.

The only responsible choice for any business application involving sensitive data.

Mastering the Craft: Prompt Engineering

The quality of your AI's output is directly proportional to the quality of your input. Using a structured framework for your prompts transforms generic responses into valuable, context-aware results. Explore the framework below.

The C.R.E.A.T.E.D. Framework

Set the stage for the AI. Tell it what persona or expertise it should adopt to provide the most relevant response.

State your primary goal clearly and directly. What is the single most important thing you want the AI to do?

Provide a concrete example of the output quality, style, or structure you're looking for. This is a powerful way to guide the AI.

Define who the final output is for and the desired tone (e.g., formal, inspirational, technical). This influences word choice and complexity.

Specify the final structure of the output. Should it be a list, an email, a table, a block of text? Provide clear formatting instructions.

Tell the AI what *not* to do. This is crucial for refining the output and avoiding unwanted information or styles.

Provide the raw information, notes, or data that the AI needs to work with to complete the task.

Example in Practice

[C] Context & Role

You are a professional copywriter specialising in compelling communications for corporate leadership development.

[R] Request & Task

Draft an email to advertise and explain a new leadership development programme.

[E] Example of Output

Here is an example sentence that captures the desired quality: "Ready to lead with confidence? Our new programme is designed to turn high-potential managers into strategic leaders."

[A] Audience & Tone

The audience is senior managers who have been identified as having high potential. The tone should be professional, inspiring, and aspirational, clearly communicating the value of the programme.

[T] Type & Format

The output must be an email with: a short, compelling subject line; a personalised greeting; 3-4 short paragraphs of body text; and a clear call to action.

[E] Exclusions

Do not use overly casual language or emojis. Do not mention the specific cost of the programme.

[D] Data Input

Use the following details for the email content:

```text
- Programme Name: Aspire Executive Leadership Programme
- Target Audience: Senior Managers (Grades 8b-8d)
- Core Modules: Strategic Finance, Leading System Change, Influencing with Impact
- Key Benefit: Prepares high-potential leaders for executive-level roles.
- Call to Action: "Click here to view the prospectus and express your interest before Friday, 15th August 2025."
```
